Chapter 1

Chapter one of this book contains ten lessons. By studying this chapter completing all the exercises with the guidance of an experienced Pāḷi teacher student will be able to develop the following skills.

  1. To have basic understanding about the Pāḷi alphabet

  2. To understand the main syntex (kāraka) of a Pāḷi sentence such as agent (subject), object, instrument and so on

  3. To know relevant nominal cases (vibhatti) to be used with regard to each kāraka

  4. To know the entire declension of the words pertaining to the nominal group of purisa

  5. To have the skill of conjugating verbs in the present tense (vattamānā vibhatti)

  6. To know how to construct simple sentences using the vattamānā vibhatti

  7. To have basic understanding on four types of words (pada) in Pāḷi

    1.1. Alphabet

    Paḷi Language: According to the Theravāda tradition, Pāḷi means the Dhamma taught by the Buddha, which is preserved as the Tipiṭaka. The language in which that Dhamma was preached is also called “Pāḷi”. As the tradition holds that language in which the Buddha expounded his teachings was called “Māgadhī,” the language spoken in the kingdom of Magadha.1

    Vowels (sara): Among the vowels, ‘e’ and ‘o’ should be pronounced as long vowels – ‘ē’ and ‘ō’. In the opinion of the Grammarian Kaccāyana, Pāli does not have short diphthongs.2 Though these letters are written as short vowels, they should be pronounced as long vowels.

    Out of the 41 letters, letters that can be pronounced alone without the help of another letter are called vowels - sara. All eight letters in the first row are vowels.

    Consonants (vyañjana): Thirty-three letters starting from the letter “k” till “ṃ” are called consonants – Vyañjana (or Byañjana).

    The last consonant “ṃ” is also called Niggahīta.




    1 Most of the modern philologers do not agree with the idea that the Buddha spoke in Māgadhī.

    2 Grammarian Moggallāna is of the opinion that Pāḷi has ten vowels with the short diphthongs. According to him, Pāḷi alphabet consists of 43 letters.

    Aspiration: Letters ‘kh, gh, ch, jh, ṭh, ḍh, th, dh, ph, bh’ are called aspirations – mahāpāṇa. An extra amount of air is used while uttering these letters.

    Note: The alphabet will be discussed in detail in coming lessons.

      Lesson 02

      1. Agent (kattu) and action (kiriyā)

        Look at the following sentence.


        Puriso gacchati – The man goes


        The agent that performs an action is called kattu (kattā) in Pāḷi. in the above sentence ‘gacchati’ (goes) is the action. Puriso – the man who does the action is its Agent or subject. An act done by the agent is called Action – kriyā in Pāḷi. In language, verbs represent actions.

        Singular and plural: Words that denote one thing are called singular words (ekavacana) while words that represent many things are called plural words (bahuvacana). In the above example, the term “puriso” denotes a single person. Therefore, it is written in the singular form. Verb ‘gacchati’ refers to the act of going done by that singular person; hence, it too is written in singular form.

        Look at the following sentence.


        Purisā gacchanti – men go


        In this sentence, the word ‘purisā’ indicates many men and the verb ‘gacchanti’ refers to the act of going done by those many men. Therefore, both the noun and the verb are written in their plural form.

Lesson 03

    1. Noun stems (liṅga) and case-endings (nāma vibhatti)

      Now you know that the agent (subject) of the sentence ‘puriso gacchati’ is ‘puriso.’ The word ‘puriso’ is made by adding the nominative case to the noun stem ‘purisa.’

      What is a noun stem (liṅga)?

      In Pāḷi, nouns are made by adding case-endings to certain morphemes. Morphemes without a case-ending are ‘noun stems.’ In Pāḷi, a noun stem is called ‘liṅga’.

      Stem words of some nouns are as follows:


      Nouns

      Stems

      Puriso

      Purisa

      Buddho

      Buddha

      Rukkho

      Rukkha

      Thero

      What is a case-ending (vibhatti)?

      Meaning of the noun stem ‘purisa’ is a man. But the stem alone cannot be used in Pāḷi sentences. In order to use the stem in a sentence, suffixes are added to it. These suffixes that are added at the end of a noun stem are called ‘case-endings’ or ‘nominal endings’ (nāma vibhatti).

      1. There are seven case-endings in Pāḷi:

        1. Paṭhamā vibhatti – nominative case

        2. Dutiyā vibhatti – accusative case

        3. Tatiyā vibhatti – instrumental case

        4. Catutthā vibhatti – dative case

        5. Pañcamā vibhatti – ablative case

        6. Chaṭṭhī vibhatti – genitive case

        7. Sattamī vibhatti – locative case

        8. Each case ending has specific meanings:

          1. Nominative case indicates the meaning of the stem of the noun.

          2. Accusative case indicates the object of an action.

          3. Instrumental case indicates the instrument of an action.

          4. Dative case indicates the dative of an action.

          5. Ablative case indicates the ablative of an action.

          6. Genitive case indicates the relation or the possessor.

          7. Locative case indicates the location of an action.

            Nominative case (paṭhamā vibhatti): Nominative case is used to indicate the mere meaning of a noun stem. In the sentence ‘puriso gacchati,’ the noun ‘puriso’ indicates ‘the man.’ It is similar to the meaning of its stem ‘purisa.’ It does not have meanings such as by the man, to the man and so on. The difference between ‘purisa’ and ‘puriso’ is that ‘purisa’ cannot be used in a sentence while ‘puriso’ can.

            Active agent or mentioned agent (utta kattu): utta means ‘something that has been said.’ Therefore, utta kattu means the agent that has been said. By whom the Agent has been said? It has been said by the verb. That is the agent of an active sentence.

            In the sentence ‘puriso gacchati – the man goes.’ ‘gacchati’ not only emphasizes the action that is being done, but also the doer of the action. By looking at the verb only we can assume, the action referred by the verb is done by some third person – not by ‘I’, ‘we’ or ‘you’. In this sentence that third person is ‘a man’ (purisa). So, in active sentences, the agent-noun is considered to have been said by the verb. Hence, Of the sentence ‘puriso gacchati,’ puriso is the said agent (utta kattu). It means the active agent. Active agent is always written in the nominative case.

            Though the active agent is written in nominative case, it should not be misunderstood that the nominative case represents the active agent. In Pāḷi, nominative case elaborates the meaning of the noun stem (liṅga) it is connected to. Explanations on other case-endings will be taught in lessons to come.


            Though the nominative case denotes the meaning of the noun stem (liṅga), active agent of a sentence is also written in nominative case.

Lesson 04

    1. Object (kamma)

      In Pāḷi grammar, the thing that is made or done by the action is called kamma. The popular rendering of the term into English is Object. Look at the following sentence.

      Buddho dhammaṃ deseti – The Buddha expounds the Dhamma.


      Of the above sentence ‘deseti’ is the verb and ‘buddho’ is the active agent (subject). What is being said (or done) by the action of expounding (deseti) is shown by the word ‘dhammaṃ.’ It is the object of the verb ‘deseti.’ Hence, dhammaṃ is the kamma of the verb deseti.

      The object is written in the accusative case. Following are two examples of the accusative case of two words.

      The place one enters, descends and ascends is also written in accusative case. For instances: Tathāgato vihāraṃ pavisati. Vānaro rukkhaṃ orohati. Nāvikā nāvaṃ orohati.


      Accusative case indicates the object of the action.


      Accusative case


      Plural

      purise (men) gāme (villiages)

      Singular purisaṃ (man) gāmaṃ (village)

      Observe how the object of following sentences are written. Regardless of the singularity or plurality of the object, the verb is written according to the active agent.

      2. Thero ganthe sajjhāyati – the elder recites the books.

      3. Dārakā ākāsaṃ olokenti – children look at the sky.

      The thing that assists the agent in executing a certain act is called the

      Instrument. In Pāḷi, it is known as karaṇa.


      The man digs the earth with a spade.

      In this sentence, spade has become the instrument for the act of digging. The case ending used to represent the instrumental is called the

      instrumental case. It is the third case ending in Pāḷi. hence, it is called

      tatiyā-vibhatti.

      Lesson 06

        1. Gender (liṅga) and addressing (ālapana)

          Stem of a noun is called ‘liṅga.’ Liṅga is the original form of a noun before case-endings are added to it. This lesson discusses about various types of liṅgas in Pāḷi grammar. At the end of the lesson, illustration will be given on ālapana – addressing or calling someone.


          Atthaliṅga (ultimate gender) and Saddaliṅga (grammatical gender)

          There is a certain uniqueness in a man’s bodily postures which enables the observers to distinguish him from a woman and eunuch (napuṃsaka). This peculiar feature in bodily postures of a man is called ‘purisa liṅga.’

          In the same manner, a different unique feature is found in the bodily posture of a female that is different from that of a male and eunuch. This trait found in female behavior is called ‘itthi liṅga.’

          A eunuch also possesses a such material feature. It is called ‘napuṃsaka liṅga.’

          These actual features found in males, females and eunuchs are called attha liṅga.

          In Pāḷi grammar, in addition to the actual gender found in reality, nouns are categorized into six groups based on their grammatical gender. This grammatical gender which has no ultimate validity like attha liṅga is called sadda liṅga.


          Six categories into which nouns are divided are as follows

          1. Purisa liṅgika (masculine nouns)

          2. Itthi liṅgika (feminine nouns)

          3. Napuṃsaka liṅgika (neuter nouns)

          4. Diliṅgika (two gender nouns)

          5. Tiliṅgika (three gender nouns)

          6. Aliṅgika (non-gender nouns)

            According to some grammarians, grammatical gender of a noun is decided on certain characteristics of the sound that is produced while uttering that particular noun. Therefore, it is not necessary for the grammatical gender

            of a noun to correspond with the ultimate gender of the thing that noun represents. Masculine nouns may refer to female or inanimate things without any gender.

            In the same way, feminine nouns may refer to males or inanimate things.

            However, largely males are referred by masculine nouns and females with feminine nouns. Nouns that represent inanimate things are scattered in all grammatical genders.

            Ālapana (addressing)

            The act of addressing or calling someone or something is called ālapana. The word used in a sentence to illustrate addressing is also called ālapana:

            1. O Buddha,

            2. Dear child,

            3. O man,

The case-ending connected to nouns indicating ālapana is also called ālapana (ālapana vibhatti). Ālapana case-ending is a sub-category of the nominative case-ending. Therefore, in Pāḷi, ālapana is not considered as a separate case-ending like nominative, accusative and so on. However, in most of the nominal declensions ālapana is given as a separate case.


Ālapana is used to denote addressing someone or something


Vocative case


Plural

(bhonto) purisā – O men!

Singular

(bho) purisa/ purisā – o man!

Words ‘bho’ and ‘bhonto’ are used in order to illustrate that the following word denotes addressing. It is not necessary to use them all the time.

Ālapana, on most occasions, stands as the second word of a sentence. It is seldom used as the first word.

Examples

  1. Ahaṃ, bho, putta, gacchāmi – O son! I go.

  2. Samaṇo, ācariya, dhammaṃ deseti – Teacher! The recluse expounds the Dhamma.

  3. Devā, bho sissa, devaloke vasanti – deities, O sudent, lives in the world of deities (heaven).

      In this lesson you will learn about the fourth and fifth case-endings in Pāḷi: Dative and Ablative respectively.


            1. Dative (sampadāna)

              The one who or the thing which receives what is being given is called the

              sampadāna. That is the indirect object. It is the dative of the action.


              The teacher gives a book to the student.


              In the above sentence, the student is the one who receives the book that is being given by the teacher. Hence, the student is the sampadāna.

              A noun that represents the sampadāna is written in the fourth case (catutthī vibhatti). In English it is called the dative case.


              The dative case is used to denote the dative of an action.


              Dative case


              Plural

              Singular

      Lesson 08

        1. Genitive (sambandha) and Location (okāsa)

          In this lesson you will learn another two nominal case endings in Pāḷī: genitive and locative.


          Genitive (sambandha)

          The word in a sentence that indicates the relation or possessor of something or someone is called genitive. It is called sambandha in Pāḷi.


          The son of the lay devotee cries.


          In the above sentence, the phrase ‘of the lay devotee’ indicates that the child who cries is related to the lay devotee. Therefore, in the point of view of Pāḷi grammarians, ‘of the lay devotee’ is the genitive or sambandha of the word ‘child.’


          The ox of the farmer runs.


          In the above sentence, the phrase ‘of the farmer’ shows the relationship between the ox and the farmer. According to it, the ox belongs to the farmer. Hence, the farmer is the possessor or genitive of the ox. This sort of relationship is called sāmī sambandha.

          In Pāḷi, sambandha is written in the sixth case-ending which is called chaṭṭhī vibhatti.

          Genitive case denotes the relation or the possessor of a noun.


          Genitive case


          Plural

          Purisānaṃ - of men, men’s

          Singular

          Purisassa – of the man, man’s


          Examples:

          1. Samaṇassa cīvaraṃ nassati – robes of the recluse get destroyed.

          2. Purisassa hattho daḍḍhati – the hand of the man gets burnt.

          3. So buddhānaṃ guṇe anussarati – he reflects the qualities of the Buddhas.

Though the forms of words in the dative case and genitive case are identical. They are completely dissimilar in terms of their meanings.


Location (okāsa or ādhāra)

The place where an action takes place is the location in Pāḷi grammar. It is called okāsa, avakāsa or ādhāra.


The monkey sits on the tree.


In the above sentence, the tree is the place where the monkey does its action of sitting. Therefore, ‘the tree’ is the location (okāsa). However, in terms of Pāḷi grammar, it should be known that the entire phrase ‘on the tree’ is considered as the location.

Location is written in the seventh case-ending, which is called sattamī vibhatti.

Locative case indicates the location where the action takes place.

      Lesson 10

        1. Verbs (Ākhyāta)

          All words in Pāḷi can be categorized into four groups.

          1. Nouns (nāmapada)

          2. Verbs (ākhyātapada)

          3. Prefixes (upasaggapada)

          4. Indeclinable (nipāta)

So far, we have already studied how nouns are declined into various cases. We also learned some of the nouns that belong to the group of purisa. Nouns can be categorized in the way they are declined in to thirteen groups.

Morphemes together with nominal case-endings (nominal suffixes) are called nouns (nāmapada). Morphemes together with verbal case-endings (verbal terminations / verbal suffixes) are called verbs (ākhyātapada). Nouns are formed by adding case-endings (nominal suffix) to noun stems. In the same way, verbs are formed by adding verbal suffixes to ‘roots.’

Roots are a collection of letters that represent a certain act. For instance, root ‘√pac’ denotes the action of cooking. Action of eating is denoted by the root √khād. Like nominal stems, roots in their original form cannot be used in a sentence. Only after adding verbal suffixes they can be utilized in a grammatical sentence. By annexing verbal suffixes to roots we get verbs. Most of the Pāḷi words origin from ‘roots.’

Noun stem + nominal suffixes > noun Root + verbal suffix (verbal termination) > verb

For example, √yā is a root that has the meaning of ‘going.’ By adding the verbal suffix ‘ti’ to it we get the verb (ākhyāta) ‘yāti.’

Altogether there are eight types of verbal suffixes (verbal terminations). Out of them, the first is called ‘vattamānā vibhatti’ – ‘present tense suffixes.’ Verbs with present tense suffixes are used to indicate present actions.


Plural

gacchanti – go, are going, do go

Singular

gacchati – goes, is going, does go

Above two verbs indicate actions that are happening at the present or actions that happens regularly. They do not represent future or past actions.


Conjugation of verbs

Like noun stems are declined based on nominal suffixes roots are conjugated based on verbal suffixes. In order to have a clear understanding about the conjugation of verbs, one should first know three types of grammatical persons.

Three types of grammatical persons

All the verbs we have discussed so far in this chapter ended either with ‘ti’ or ‘nti.’ They are the verbal endings of the third person (paṭhama-purisa). There are two other grammatical persons related to verbs: second person (majjhima-purisa) and first person (uttama-purisa).

When the subject is ‘I’ or ‘we’, the verbal termination has to be ‘mi’ and ‘ma’ respectively.


Ahaṃ gacchāmi – I go Mayaṃ gacchāma – we go

When the subject is ‘you (singular)’ or ‘you (plural)’, the verb ends with ‘si’ and ‘tha’ respectively.

Tvaṃ gacchasi – you go Tumhe gacchatha – you go

When the subject is something or someone other than ‘I’, ‘we’ or ‘you’, the verb ends with ‘ti’ for singular subjects and ‘nti’ for plural subjects.


Puriso gacchati – a man goes Purisā gacchanti – men go

The verb ‘gacchāmi’ expresses that the act of going is done by ‘me.’ The verb ‘gacchāma’ expresses that the act of going is done by ‘us.’

The verb ‘gacchasi’ expresses that the act of going is done by singular ‘you.’

The verb ‘gacchatha’ expresses that the act of going is done by plural ‘you.’

The verb ‘gacchati’ expresses that the act of going is done by a singular subject other than ‘me’ and ‘you.’

The verb ‘gacchanti’ expresses that the act of going is done by a plural subject other than ‘us’ and ‘you.’

    1. Indeclinable particles (nipāta) and prefixes (upasagga)

All words in Pāḷi language are classified into four categories: nāma (nouns), ākhyāta (verbs), nipāta (indeclinable particles) and upasagga (prefixes). So far, we have briefly studied about nouns and verbs. In this lesson, we shall look into indeclinables and prefixes.

Nipātapada (indeclinable particles)

Words that express various meanings but do not change their forms according to case-endings and numbers they are connected with are called nipāta (indeclinable particles).

Grammatically, nipāta is declined. However, their form does not undergo any changes as in other nouns. Therefore, the English rendering ‘indeclinable’ is given to them based their nature of not changing their forms in declension.

The nipāta word ‘evaṃ’ means ‘thus’ or ‘in this manner.’ It does not undergo any changes as the word ‘purisa’ while it is being declined in ‘all cases and numbers.’ The word ‘evaṃ’ retains its original form in any place in a sentence regard less of the words it is connected to.

Followings are four nipātas and their example sentences.


evaṃ - thus, in this manner


e.g., Buddho evaṃ dhammaṃ deseti. heṭṭhā – under

e.g., Sunakho mañcassa heṭṭhā niddāyati. ajja – today

e.g., Ahaṃ ajja pajānāmi. puna – again

e.g., Moggallānatthero puna devalokaṃ gacchati.


Prefixes (upasagga)

Prefixes also do not undergo any changes during declension. In most of the cases, they are added to the beginning of a root or a noun. Prefixes change the meaning of the respective root and noun they are prefixed to.

‘upa’ is a prefix that indicates ‘closeness.’ When it is prefixed to the root ‘√gam – to go’, the verb constructed appears as upgacchati. Its meaning is ‘approaches close’.

When the same prefix is added to the beginning of the noun ‘nagara’ –‘city’, The newly constructed noun appears as upanagara. Its meaning is ‘close to the city’.

There are twenty prefixes (upasagga) in Pāḷi.


pa, parā, ni, nī, u, du, saṃ, vi, ava, anu, pari, adhi, abhi, pati, su, ā, ati, api, apa and upa.”


Below are few examples of words with prefixes.

pajānāti, parājayo, nimmalaṃ, nīharati, upagacchati, dussīlo, saṃsāro, viharati.
